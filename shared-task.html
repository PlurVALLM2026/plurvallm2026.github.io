Shared Task

Benchmark suite & shared task

The shared task evaluates LLMs’ value alignment and normative reasoning across adversarial, daily, and principle-driven settings. It is organized around three benchmarks in Indonesian, Sri Lankan (English + Sinhala), and Chinese (the Chinese track may use attack-style or scenario-style setups comparable to the other two). The competition platform, full timeline, and registration will be announced later.

  • Evaluation protocol — a dedicated scoring framework for the task.
  • Baselines — reference implementations or scores for comparison.
  • Evaluation code — release policy will be announced.

Dataset statistics below come from benchmark papers and proposals; final rules may differ from what is on this page.

D2VBench

Chinese · scenario-class everyday dilemmas

Built on a four-layer hierarchical value system (Livelihood, Social Order, Civilization) with 158 fine-grained leaf concepts and on the order of ~10k instances. Scenarios are constructed through a four-stage pipeline (concept inventory → generation → quality control → reference & judge calibration), with Align-Score, five-dimensional rationale quality, a structured reason bank, and alignment metrics against human and referee models—suited for normative reasoning beyond single-label classification. A system-level proposal also targets on the order of ~20k scenario samples with free-form responses vs. human-curated references.

Indonesian · Pancasila-Dilemmas

Indonesia · multiple-choice value dilemmas

1,834 four-option items grounded in the Pancasila five principles, sourced from Kompas headlines with crowd and expert validation. Evaluation can report accuracy and distributional agreement (e.g. JSD / TVD), including majoritarian vs. “any-agreement” style metrics against human majorities or full distributions.

LKvaluesBench (Sri Lanka)

Sri Lanka · bilingual judgment alignment

2,000 instances in English and Sinhala (paired translations) over 40 survey-derived societal values. Labels indicate which language carries the judgment (A / B / BOTH / neither). Reports model–human alignment and cross-lingual consistency—testing whether “alignment” transfers across languages within one cultural context.

Detailed task description, data specifications, baselines, and leaderboards will be released with the official shared-task announcement. Participation is expected from on the order of 10–20 teams (proposal estimate), with results presented in a dedicated session.
